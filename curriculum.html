Chapter One

The Method

Speak Easy 4 Kids is not a curriculum. It is a rhythm. Six thematic sessions rotate through the school year; every week within a session follows the same predictable arc, so kids never wonder what happens next — they wonder which words they'll get to play with today.

Six principles that never change

01 · Played, not studied

Every vocabulary point is delivered as a game. Pictionary, charades, BINGO, scavenger hunts, parachute drops, embodied miming. The prize is the vocabulary. Kids stop noticing they are learning.

02 · Speak Easy Dollars

The classroom's token economy. Kids earn for showing up, speaking unprompted, catching pronunciation mistakes, helping a peer. They spend on rounds, prizes, hand stamps, or a chance to lead the next game. The economy is what turns risk into fun.

03 · Ritual over variety

Kids need the same opening every week (Greeting Gate → Feelings Wheel → Parachute). Predictable structure lowers cognitive load. That freed load is exactly what a second language needs to land.

04 · Culture inside the words

You do not teach queso. You teach how to build a pizza in Spanish — sitting in a V-stretch on the floor, miming cheese onto your legs. Vocabulary anchored to lived experience sticks.

05 · No anonymous students

By Week 2, every kid knows every other kid's name. The Greeting Gate makes it non-optional. The Friend Bench and the Calm Space give shy or overwhelmed kids a place to be — with language for asking.

06 · Regulation as content

One breath technique per session (belly, flower, volcano, buddy, cookie, balloon). One animal stretch anchor (dog, cat, butterfly, frog, tree, lion). Kids leave with Spanish AND a nervous-system toolkit. Both are the curriculum.

Chapter Two

Weekly Class Rhythm

Sixty minutes. Same order every week. Different vocabulary and different games inside the blocks. This is the operating system.

0–5 min
Greeting GateKids arrive one at a time and must greet the instructor + at least two peers by name in Spanish before joining the circle. First week: heavy prompting. By Week 4: automatic.
5–10 min
Feelings Wheel Check-InEach kid points to how they feel on the Spanish feelings wheel — feliz, triste, cansado/a, emocionado/a. They say the word out loud. Anchors emotional vocabulary to real states.
10–15 min
Parachute OpenerEveryone under the parachute. Instructor calls categories — "Todos los que tienen un hermano, corran debajo!" — and the correct kids run underneath. Categories double as vocabulary review.
15–35 min
Theme + GameThe week's vocabulary, taught through a game. This is the core learning block. Never a worksheet. Pictionary, BINGO, charades, matching, embodied learning.
35–45 min
Regulation BreakThe session's breath (belly / flower / volcano / buddy / cookie / balloon) plus the session's animal stretch. Two to three minutes of quiet nervous-system reset.
45–55 min
Embodied PracticeKids USE the words through movement: Somos Pizza, Somos Ropa, Somos Animales. Vocabulary + proprioception in the same second.
55–60 min
Closing Ritual + Take-HomeSpeak Easy Dollars distributed. Take-home sheet with the week's vocab + pronunciation guide + family reinforcement suggestions. Goodbye Song. Passport stamp.

Never let the rhythm collapse to save time. If you skip the Greeting Gate, kids notice, and the "we're a class" feeling erodes for the whole hour. The predictability is the pedagogy.

Chapter Three

Social Infrastructure

Four fixtures in the room. Introduce all four on Day 1. They are the difference between a class and a community.

The Greeting Gate

The physical space at the classroom entrance where every kid must greet before joining. The instructor stands here Week 1 and Week 2. From Week 3 on, a rotating student greeter holds the role. Non-negotiable.

¡Hola, [name]!Hi, [name]!
¿Cómo estás hoy?How are you today?
Estoy bien / cansado / emocionadoI'm good / tired / excited

The Feelings Wheel

Poster-sized wheel on the wall with six illustrated Spanish emotions: feliz, triste, enojado/a, asustado/a, cansado/a, emocionado/a. Every kid points to theirs at check-in. Instructor references it throughout the class ("¿Estás cansada después de la carrera?").

Banco de Amigos — The Friend Bench

A small designated spot (a bench, a rug, a chair pair) where a kid can go if they feel lonely, left out of a game, or without a partner. Any kid who sees someone on the bench must go over, greet them, and become their partner for the next activity.

"¿Quieres ser mi amigo/a?" · "Estoy solo/a" · "Voy al Banco de Amigos"

Teaches kids that loneliness is speakable, and that seeing loneliness in someone else creates a small responsibility to respond.

Espacio Tranquilo — The Calm Space

A separate quiet corner (cushion + soft fabric + optional fidget). A kid can go there when they're overwhelmed, overstimulated, or need to be alone. No shame. No time limit. Instructor checks in with a soft "¿Estás mejor?" after a few minutes.

"Necesito estar tranquilo/a" · "Voy al Espacio Tranquilo" · "Estoy agitado/a"

Teaches self-regulation as an available option, in Spanish, that a kid can request without argument.

Names by Week 2

Week 1: circle intro; every kid says their name; class repeats it three times. Poster with every name goes up on the wall. Week 2: greetings must be by name. By Week 3, the room is not anonymous — and it never becomes anonymous again.

Chapter Four

Regulation Toolkit

One breathing technique per session. Kids who cycle through all six sessions leave with a small nervous-system toolkit they can use anywhere.

A

Belly Breath (Respiración de barriga)

Hand on belly, breathe in — belly balloons out. Breathe out — belly goes flat. Six slow rounds. Foundation. Also pairs with the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding scan.

B

Flower Breath (Respiración de flor)

Cupped hands as a flower to the nose. Inhale the scent — long, slow. Exhale — "aaaah". Great for calming after intense play. Pair with tactile scent anchoring.

C

Volcano Breath (Respiración de volcán)

Hands together at heart. Inhale — arms up over head. Exhale — hands "erupt" out and down. Discharges energy. Pair with shake-it-out and power poses.

D

Buddy Breath (Respiración con amigo/a)

Partners sit knee-to-knee. Match breathing to the other person for six rounds. Teaches co-regulation. Pair with partner mirror-stretch.

E

Cookie Breath (Respiración de galleta)

Imagine holding a warm cookie. Inhale the smell. Exhale to cool it down (long slow "whoooo"). Trains focus and long exhale — vagal activation.

F

Balloon Breath (Respiración de globo)

Arms out to sides. Inhale — arms rise, chest expands like a balloon. Exhale slow — arms sink, balloon deflates. Full-body integration. Grounding.

Introduce Week 1. Repeat Weeks 2–5 as needed (before or after high-energy blocks). Week 6 shows off the toolkit to families as part of the finale.

Chapter Six

Weekly Manners Ladder

One politeness phrase highlighted each week. Instructor models it constantly. Kids get Speak Easy Dollars for using it unprompted. By Week 6, all six are in circulation.

Week 2 · Por favor / DisculpaPlease / Excuse me

Chapter Seven

Embodied Learning Library

The vocabulary lands in the body, not just the ear. These are the signature Speak Easy moves.

Somos Pizza — We Are Pizza

Kids sit in a V-stretch on the floor, legs long. Instructor calls out ingredients in Spanish — ¡Queso! ¡Tomate! ¡Cebolla! ¡Jalapeños! Kids mime spreading each ingredient across their legs and torso. The V-sit doubles as a hamstring stretch. Ingredients get repeated many times without it feeling like drill.

Somos Ropa — We Are Clothes

Kids stand. Instructor calls clothing vocabulary — ¡Sombrero! — kids mime putting on a hat. ¡Zapatos! — they mime tying shoes. ¡Chaqueta! — jacket, arms through sleeves. Vocabulary + gross motor + laughter.

Somos Animales — We Are Animals

Instructor calls an animal in Spanish; kids do the sound + a stretch that fits. ¡Gato! = cat-cow stretch and meow. ¡Rana! = frog squat and ribbit. Doubles as the animal-stretch review.

Parachute Colors

Kids hold the parachute edge. Instructor calls a color in Spanish. Kids holding that section lift high while chanting the color. Advance the game with compound calls: ¡Tres rojos! (three reds lift), ¡Verde salta! (green jumps while holding).

Restaurant Role-Play

One kid is the server, others are customers. Menu is week's food vocabulary. Kids order in Spanish — "Un taco por favor" — and pay in Speak Easy Dollars.

Chapter Eight

The Six Sessions

A full school year is six 6-week sessions. Kids can enroll in any single session; the ones who cycle through all six get the whole toolkit. Session A is the foundation and is detailed in the next chapter. Sessions B–F share the same structure with rotating themes.

A

Foundation

  • Numbers 1–10
  • Colors
  • Weather
  • Animals
  • Clothing basics
  • Body parts + greetings

Breath: Belly. Stretch: Perro.

B

Motion & Place

  • Transportation
  • Beach vocabulary
  • Action verbs (correr, saltar, nadar)
  • Numbers 11–15
  • Weather + seasons

Breath: Flor. Stretch: Gato-vaca.

C

Feelings & Home

  • Feelings vocabulary (expanded)
  • Foods (breakfast, snacks)
  • Clothing (expanded)
  • House vocabulary
  • Family expanded (aunt, uncle, cousin)

Breath: Volcán. Stretch: Mariposa.

D

Together

  • Numbers 16–20
  • Partner activities
  • Sports
  • Fruits
  • Opposites (grande/pequeño, etc.)

Breath: Con amigo. Stretch: Rana.

E

School & Days

  • Body parts (expanded with Macarena)
  • Vegetables
  • Days of the week
  • Classroom items
  • Animals (round two)

Breath: Galleta. Stretch: Árbol.

F

Time & Celebration

  • Months of the year (Macarena)
  • Numbers 20–30
  • Weather (expanded)
  • Holidays & festivals
  • Storytelling vocabulary

Breath: Globo. Stretch: León.

Every session, Week 4 is Piñata Week — a mid-session celebration that kids remember and tell parents about. Every session, Week 6 is the Finale Showcase — families invited to see what their kids can do.

Chapter Nine

Session A · In Detail

The foundation session. Every certified instructor teaches this one first. It establishes every ritual, every phrase set, and every classroom fixture that every subsequent session inherits.

Week 1
¡Hola! · Numbers 1–10 + Names
Vocabulary
Uno, dos, tres, cuatro, cinco, seis, siete, ocho, nueve, diez. Mi nombre es… Me llamo… ¡Mucho gusto!
Game
Hot Potato with a soft ball. Whoever holds the ball on the beat says "Me llamo [name]" and passes.
Manners phrase
Gracias / De nada.
Introduce today
Greeting Gate · Feelings Wheel · Banco de Amigos · Espacio Tranquilo · Speak Easy Dollars.
Take-home
Numbers 1–10 pronunciation card + a page of family reinforcement suggestions.
Week 2
Los Colores · Colors
Vocabulary
Rojo, azul, amarillo, verde, negro, blanco, rosa, morado.
Game
Twister with color names in Spanish. Also parachute colors — lift when your color is called.
Manners phrase
Por favor / Disculpa.

By end of Week 2, every kid greets every other kid by name. This is enforced at the Greeting Gate.
Take-home
Colors flashcard sheet + a suggestion for a family "color hunt" walk.
Week 3
El Tiempo · Weather + Open/Close
Vocabulary
Sol, lluvia, nieve, viento, calor, frío. Abre, cierra.
Game

Manners phrase
¿Puedo…?
Take-home
A weekly weather chart. Kids track each day of the week in Spanish for the household to see.
Week 4 · Piñata Week
Los Animales · Animals + PIÑATA celebration
Vocabulary
Gato, perro, pájaro, pez, conejo, caballo, vaca, oveja.
Game
Old MacDonald in Spanish. Animal charades. Puppet parade.
Manners phrase
¡Bien hecho!
Piñata block
Mid-session cultural celebration. A small piñata with wrapped candies + review-vocabulary slips inside. Kids take turns swinging while class chants "¡Dale, dale, dale!" Prizes trigger a mid-session buzz that families hear about at home — the point is to drive re-enrollment by making Week 4 the story the kid brings home.
Take-home
Animal vocabulary sheet + a story starter: "In our language, dogs say 'woof.' In Spanish they say 'guau guau.'"
Week 5
La Ropa · Clothing
Vocabulary
Camisa, pantalones, zapatos, sombrero, chaqueta, vestido.
Game
Somos Ropa (embodied). Felt-board dressing game with prop clothes. Clothesline race where teams peg items after hearing the Spanish word.
Manners phrase
¿Cómo estás?
Take-home
Clothing labels sheet — kids label items in their own room in Spanish for a week.
Week 6 · Finale
El Showcase · Body parts + greetings + performance
Vocabulary
Cabeza, ojos, nariz, boca, orejas, hombros, rodillas, pies. Plus a greeting review.
Game
Cabeza · Hombros · Rodillas y Pies (Head · Shoulders · Knees · Toes in Spanish). Hokey Pokey.
Manners phrase
Adiós · Hasta luego.
Showcase block
Families invited in the second half of class. Kids perform three things: a greeting round-robin with each family member, one game demonstration, and one embodied learning bit (Somos Pizza is the crowd-pleaser). Certificates + passport stamps + a farewell photo. Enrollment pitch for Session B goes home in the take-home packet.
Take-home
Passport with all six stamps + a certificate + a flyer for Session B.

Sessions B–F follow this exact rhythm. The Piñata always lands on Week 4. The Finale always lands on Week 6. Only the vocabulary themes rotate.

Chapter Ten

The Week-4 Piñata Protocol

The piñata is not decoration. It is the enrollment engine for the next session. When a kid comes home telling their parents about the piñata, the parent registers for Session B before Week 6 pricing goes up.

Materials

  • One small piñata (~$8, any store-bought)
  • Wrapped candies
  • Slips of paper with review-vocabulary from Weeks 1–3 (each slip = one review word)
  • A blindfold (optional; younger kids skip)
  • A soft foam bat

The Ritual

  1. Explain the cultural origin. Piñatas come from Mexico and Central America. This is part of the language — not just a party.
  2. Load the piñata with candies AND vocabulary slips.
  3. Class chants "¡Dale, dale, dale, no pierdas el tino!" as each kid takes their swing (older kids blindfolded, younger kids not).
  4. When it breaks — every kid grabs. Each candy comes with a vocabulary slip. Kids read their slip out loud to claim the candy.
  5. Group photo. Send home with the take-home sheet.
Chapter Eleven

The Week-6 Finale Showcase

Families invited. Class runs 90 minutes instead of 60 for this week only. The goal is threefold: kids show their skills, families see the method, and Session B enrollment closes.

Run of Show

0–15 min
Normal class opening (kids only)Greeting Gate + Feelings Wheel + parachute — a warm-up families arrive during, so they see the ritual in action.
15–20 min
Family greeting circleEvery kid introduces their family in Spanish. "Ella es mi mamá. Se llama…"
20–40 min
Game demoKids run one of the games from a prior week — parents play along in Spanish.
40–55 min
Embodied learning bitSomos Pizza is the crowd-pleaser. Everyone V-sits and mimes toppings. Loud laughter is normal and desired.
55–70 min
Certificates + passport stampsEach kid gets their achievement certificate + final passport stamp. Photos.
70–90 min
Instructor Q&A + Session B enrollmentInstructor answers parent questions. Session B info sheets are on a table. Sibling discount promoted.
Chapter Twelve

Take-Home Sheets

Every week, every kid leaves with a single sheet. The sheet is the interface between the classroom and the household. The goal is to give parents enough that they can reinforce without needing to speak Spanish themselves.

Standard sheet layout

  • Top: Week number + theme name in big Spanish letters
  • Vocabulary block: This week's 5–8 words with pronunciation guide in parentheses. Ex: gato (GAH-toh) — cat.
  • Manners phrase of the week: Bold, its own line.
  • Suggestions for home: 3–4 casual reinforcement ideas. Ex: "At dinner, ask your child to name the food in Spanish before eating it."
  • Song/game we did: Name it so a parent can ask about it.
  • Passport stamp box: Empty box for the kid to get stamped at next week's class.
Chapter Thirteen

Recognition & Certificates

Two mid-session, one end-of-session. Cost per certificate is essentially zero — printer paper and cardstock. The behavioral value is enormous.

Good Conduct Award

Given ad-hoc to kids who have shown a specific behavioral shift — the kid who was disruptive but had a quieter week, the kid who used the Friend Bench well, the kid who chose the Calm Space instead of escalating. Not a participation trophy. Named on the certificate: "Given to [Name] for using the Espacio Tranquilo when he needed to."

Achievement Award

Given for concrete language milestones. First unprompted full sentence in Spanish. First peer they taught a word to. Best pronunciation of the week. Also given for helping — teaching, translating, encouraging.

Session Completion Certificate

End of Week 6. Every kid gets one. Signed by the instructor. Names the specific session ("has completed Session A: Foundation"). Doubles as a re-enrollment prompt — parents keep them, and the next session sticker is the invitation.

Per-session specials

Optional but recommended

  • Bluetooth speaker for songs and Macarena
  • Instant camera for finale photos
  • Simple snack for tasting circles (Week 3 in some sessions)
